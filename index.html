Rethinking Urban Life in a Post-Commute Canada

Published November 27, 2025 • By Alex Harding

As remote work settles into the cultural mainstream, Canadian cities are being forced to confront an uncomfortable question: what is a downtown core without the daily rush of commuters? For decades, our urban planning philosophies have revolved around the assumption that workers must travel to a central hub.

But now, as more companies embrace distributed work models, the incentives that once shaped our skylines are shifting. Office towers—long symbols of productivity—sit half-empty during peak hours, and cafés built for business crowds face disappearing foot traffic.

“This is a generational moment,” says one urban policy expert. “We can either wait for a return to the past, or we can design cities for the lives Canadians actually live today.”

Some municipalities are exploring bold adaptive strategies: converting unused commercial floors into residential units, expanding green spaces, and redesigning transit systems for flexibility rather than funneling. Whether these plans will succeed depends on political will—and whether citizens can imagine a new social contract with their cities.
